Getting your first tattoo is exciting, but it is normal to have questions. What do you send when booking? How much detail should you know already? What happens at the appointment? How bad does it hurt? What if you do not know the exact style name?

You do not need to walk in speaking fluent tattoo-shop. You just need a clear starting point, a little preparation, and an artist whose work fits the tattoo you want.

Here is what to expect when planning your first tattoo at United Tattoo in Fountain, Colorado.

## Start with the idea, not the perfect design

You do not need a finished design before reaching out. In most cases, the artist should be the one turning your idea into something tattooable.

A strong starting point can be:

- The subject of the tattoo.
- The mood or meaning behind it.
- The style you like.
- A few reference images.
- The placement.
- A rough size range.
- Whether you want black and grey or color.

For example, "a fine line birth flower on my forearm" is enough to start a conversation. So is "a black and grey memorial portrait of my dog" or "traditional flash with bold color."

If you are not sure what style your idea belongs to, that is okay. Browse the [artist pages](/artists) and look for work that feels close.

## Choose the artist by style fit

The best first tattoo experience usually starts with the right artist match. Every artist has a different visual language. Some are strongest in fine line, some in traditional, some in realism, some in color, some in script, and some in illustrative work.

Before booking, look at portfolios for:

- Similar subject matter.
- Similar line weight.
- Similar use of color or shading.
- Clean healed work if available.
- Tattoos on placements similar to yours.

At United Tattoo, the roster covers a wide range of styles, including fine line, botanical, geometric, American traditional, black and grey, realism, portraits, watercolor, illustrative, Japanese-inspired work, neo-traditional, color realism, and cartoon-style tattoos.

If you are unsure who fits your idea, send the idea anyway. The studio can help point you in the right direction.

## What to include in your booking request

A good booking request saves time and helps the artist understand the tattoo faster.

Include:

- Your preferred artist, if you have one.
- A short description of the tattoo idea.
- Placement on the body.
- Approximate size in inches.
- Black and grey or color.
- Reference images.
- Any existing tattoos near the placement.
- Your general availability.
- Any important deadline, like travel, events, or military scheduling.

Try to avoid sending twenty unrelated references. A few good references are better than a giant camera roll avalanche.

The goal is not to copy another tattoo. The goal is to show the artist what you like about the style, layout, subject, or mood.

## What happens before the appointment?

Depending on the tattoo, there may be a consultation, a deposit, or direct scheduling. Larger custom work usually needs more conversation than a small flash piece.

Before the appointment, your artist may clarify:

- Size.
- Placement.
- Style direction.
- Budget range.
- Session length.
- Whether the piece needs multiple sessions.
- Whether the idea is realistic for the placement.

This is also your time to ask questions. If you are nervous, say so. Good artists are used to first-timers and would rather answer early than have you quietly panic in the chair.

## How to prepare the day before

The basics matter more than people think.

The day before your tattoo:

- Get real sleep if you can.
- Drink water.
- Avoid heavy drinking.
- Do not sunburn the area.
- Do not shave unless your artist tells you to.
- Make sure you know where you are going and when to arrive.
- Eat normally.

A tattoo appointment is easier when your body is not running on one iced coffee and spite.

## What to bring to your first tattoo appointment

Bring the practical stuff:

- Valid ID.
- Water.
- A small snack for longer sessions.
- Headphones if you want them.
- Comfortable clothing that gives access to the tattoo area.
- A phone charger if it is a longer appointment.
- Any final reference notes your artist requested.

Wear clothes that can handle possible ink or stencil transfer. If you are getting your thigh tattooed, do not wear skinny jeans. If you are getting your ribs tattooed, think about coverage and comfort. Your future self will appreciate the planning.

## What happens when you arrive?

Your artist will usually confirm the design, size, and placement. They may print or adjust the stencil, clean the area, place the stencil, and let you check it in the mirror.

Take stencil placement seriously. This is the moment to speak up if something feels off.

It is okay to ask for small placement changes. It is better to adjust the stencil than to spend years politely resenting your own forearm.

Once the placement is approved, the artist will set up the station and begin tattooing.

## How much does a first tattoo hurt?

Tattoo pain depends on placement, size, style, session length, and your own pain tolerance. Most people handle it better than they expect, but it is still a tattoo. It will feel like something.

Common easier placements include outer arm, forearm, outer thigh, and calf. More sensitive spots often include ribs, sternum, feet, hands, elbows, knees, and areas close to bone or with thin skin.

Pain also changes during the appointment. The first few minutes can feel intense because your body is figuring out what is happening. Then many people settle into it.

If you need a break, ask. Do not try to become a silent statue of masculine folklore. Communicating is normal.

## What happens after the tattoo is finished?

Your artist will clean the tattoo, may take photos, and will cover or wrap it according to their aftercare process. They will explain how to care for it and when to remove the covering.

Listen carefully to aftercare instructions. Different artists may use different bandages or healing methods, so follow the instructions you were given for your tattoo.

You should also plan the rest of the day around the fact that you have a fresh tattoo. Avoid swimming, heavy sweating, dirty environments, tight clothing over the tattoo, and direct sun while it starts healing.

## How long does a tattoo take to heal?

The surface of a tattoo often starts settling over the first couple weeks, but deeper healing takes longer. During healing, you may see redness, tenderness, peeling, flaking, and itchiness.

Do not pick at it. Let the tattoo heal.

If you are worried about something, contact the studio or your artist. It is better to ask than to guess.

## First tattoo mistakes to avoid

Avoid these if you can:

- Choosing an artist only because they are available soonest.
- Making a detailed design too small.
- Getting tattooed while sunburned.
- Showing up hungry or dehydrated.
- Wearing clothing that blocks the tattoo area.
- Copying someone else's custom tattoo exactly.
- Ignoring aftercare.
- Being afraid to ask questions.

The best first tattoo is not always the biggest or most dramatic. It is the one that fits you, fits the artist, and is planned well enough to heal into something you still like later.

## First tattoo FAQ

### Do I need to know exactly what I want?

No. You need a starting point. Bring the subject, placement, size range, and references. Your artist can help shape the final tattoo.

### Can I bring a friend?

Ask the studio before your appointment. Space and policies can vary, and some appointments are easier with fewer people around.

### Should I use numbing cream?

Ask your artist before using anything on the skin. Some products can affect the tattoo process or skin texture.

### Can I get tattooed before a vacation?

Be careful. Fresh tattoos need time away from sun, swimming, soaking, and heavy friction. If your vacation involves water or sun, schedule the tattoo after the trip.
