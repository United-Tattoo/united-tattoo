Booking a tattoo should not feel like solving a puzzle before you even talk to an artist. You should be able to share the idea, send a few references, choose the artist who feels right, and get the conversation started without calling around or wondering what details matter.

That is why United Tattoo uses an online booking form for tattoo requests near Colorado Springs and Fountain, Colorado. The form is built around the same things an artist needs to understand your project: the style, placement, size, references, timing, and contact details.

You do not need the whole tattoo figured out before you reach out. You just need a useful starting point.

## Start with the artist, if you have one in mind

If you already know who you want to work with, choose that artist on the booking form. This helps route the request clearly from the beginning.

If you are not sure yet, that is fine too. You can browse the [artist portfolios](/artists) first, or choose no preference and let the studio help point the request in the right direction.

When comparing artists, look for:

- Work that feels close to your idea.
- Similar use of line weight, color, or shading.
- Tattoos in a style you can imagine wearing.
- Portfolios that make you feel confident about the direction.

You do not have to know every tattoo style name. If you can tell what you like when you see it, that is enough to start.

## Choose the style that feels closest

The booking form asks for a tattoo style so the studio can understand the general direction. This might be traditional, fine line, black and grey, realism, botanical, geometric, or something else.

If none of the options feel exact, choose the closest one and explain the rest in the description. Tattoo ideas are rarely perfect dropdown-menu objects. A short note like "mostly fine line, but with soft shading" or "traditional feel, but less color" can be more helpful than trying to force the idea into one label.

## Add the placement and approximate size

Placement and size matter because tattoos are designed for skin, movement, and long-term readability.

For placement, be specific when you can:

- Inner forearm.
- Outer upper arm.
- Back of calf.
- Sternum.
- Left shoulder blade.
- Above the knee.

For size, an estimate is enough. "Palm sized," "about 4 inches," "half sleeve," or "small enough to fit above my ankle" all give the artist a starting point.

If the idea needs to be larger or simpler to heal well, the artist can talk through that with you.

## Send references, but keep them focused

Reference images are useful because they show the mood, layout, subject, or style you are drawn to. They do not need to be perfect.

A few focused references are better than twenty unrelated screenshots. Try to include images that answer different questions:

- One for the subject.
- One for the style.
- One for the layout or placement.
- One for color, if color matters.

It also helps to explain what you like about each reference. For example: "I like the flower shape in the first image, the line weight in the second, and the placement in the third."

The goal is not to copy someone else's tattoo. The goal is to give your artist enough visual language to design something that fits you.

## Write the description like you are talking to a person

The description box is where you can explain the idea in plain language. It does not need to sound polished.

Useful details include:

- What the tattoo should be.
- Whether it has personal meaning.
- If you want black and grey or color.
- Whether you are working around existing tattoos.
- What you do not want included.
- Any deadline or scheduling constraint.

For example:

"I want a fine line birth flower tattoo on my inner forearm, around 4 inches. I like delicate linework but still want it to heal cleanly. I attached a few references for the flower shape and placement. I am open to artist suggestions."

That is a strong booking request. It is clear, realistic, and easy to respond to.

## Share when you are available

The form also asks about preferred dates and times. You do not need to know the exact appointment yet. General availability helps the studio avoid unnecessary back-and-forth.

Examples:

- Weekends work best.
- Tuesday or Thursday afternoons.
- After 4pm on weekdays.
- I am flexible, but need two weeks notice.
- I am visiting Colorado Springs in June.

If your schedule is tight, say that early. If you are flexible, say that too.

## What happens after you send the request?

After you submit the form, the studio reviews the details and follows up with the next step. Depending on the tattoo, that may mean clarifying the idea, confirming the artist fit, discussing scheduling, or setting up a consultation.

Some tattoos are simple enough to move quickly. Larger custom pieces, cover-ups, sleeves, or detailed work may need more conversation first.

That is normal. The point of the form is to give the studio enough information to respond with something useful instead of starting from zero.

## A better request usually gets a better response

You do not need to write a novel. You do not need to know the final design. You do not need to have perfect references.

But a clear request helps everyone:

- The artist understands the project faster.
- The studio can route the request better.
- You spend less time repeating the same details.
- The first reply can focus on the tattoo, not basic questions.

If you have the idea, placement, rough size, references, and availability, you have enough to begin.
